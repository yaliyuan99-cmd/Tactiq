

Tactiq — Full Project Summary

What it is
Tactiq is a wearable gesture-based phone control system built around two smart rings — one worn on each hand. Together, the ten fingers and their knuckle joints form a complete, always-available input interface that lets users control their phone entirely through touch, without looking at a screen, speaking a word, or reaching for a button.
The interface lives on the body. The hands are the controller.

How it works
Each hand wears one ring, positioned to detect tap pressure and knuckle flex across all five fingers. The two rings communicate with each other and with the user's phone via Bluetooth, translating finger gestures into phone commands in real time.
The right hand — the command hand
The three middle fingers form a 3x3 grid. Each finger has three knuckle positions, giving nine distinct tap points that the user assigns to their most-used actions — skipping audio, opening navigation, sending a voice message, adjusting volume, calling a contact, or anything else they choose.
The thumb carries two fixed system functions. The top knuckle capitalises or shifts input. The bottom knuckle switches language or input mode. The thumb can also be held down as a live modifier, unlocking a second layer of nine commands across the same grid — doubling the available shortcuts without adding complexity.
The pinky handles deletion and personal shortcuts. The top knuckle is permanently fixed as the delete cluster — one tap deletes a character, two taps delete a word, three taps clear the entire field. This is fixed for every user, always in the same place, always reliable. The middle and bottom pinky knuckles are fully customisable, each carrying three actions through tap count, giving six personal shortcut slots on the pinky alone.
The left hand — the navigation and input hand
The left hand mirrors the right hand's physical architecture but serves a different role. It handles directional navigation, media control, accessibility shortcuts, and any overflow commands the user wants to assign. For users who type using the knuckle grid, the two hands can work in combination — the right hand on content input, the left hand on editing and navigation — creating a full two-handed input system that rivals a physical keyboard in speed once muscle memory develops.
Total command capacity
Across both hands, with tap counts and thumb modifier layers, Tactiq supports well over 60 distinct gestures from natural hand anatomy alone — all accessible without a screen, without a voice, and without looking at anything.

The problem it solves
Smartphones are the most important personal device most people own. For blind and visually impaired users, they are not a convenience — they are essential infrastructure for independence. Navigation, communication, banking, medical appointments, transport, emergency contact — all of it runs through a phone.
And yet the phone remains fundamentally hostile to blind users. Touchscreens give no physical feedback. Menus are buried. Repeated actions require multiple gestures every single time. The phone was designed by sighted people, for sighted people, and accessibility was added afterward as an afterthought rather than built in from the start.
Voice control is the standard answer. It is an inadequate one. Voice assistants fail in noisy environments — on public transport, in busy streets, in shared workspaces. They fail users whose accents, dialects, or speech patterns the system does not recognise accurately. They require the user to speak aloud in situations where that is impractical, embarrassing, or simply impossible. They introduce a delay — wake word, pause, command, response — between the user's intention and any action. And they fail entirely for users with speech impairments, who are disproportionately represented in the blind and visually impaired community.
The result is that blind users spend significantly more time and cognitive effort on basic phone tasks than sighted users. That gap accumulates every day, across every interaction, for every year they own a phone.

The accessibility crisis in assistive technology
Here is the reality that most product conversations avoid: professional assistive technology is extraordinarily expensive. Braille displays — devices that translate screen text into physical Braille — cost between $1,500 and $7,000. Screen reader software licences run hundreds of dollars per year. Specialised accessible phones and devices sit at price points that place them beyond most individuals without institutional support. In many countries, government funding for assistive technology is limited, slow to access, bureaucratically complex, and frequently insufficient.
The people who need these tools most are also, statistically, among those least likely to be able to afford them. Blindness and visual impairment disproportionately affect older adults and people in lower-income populations globally. The assistive technology market has historically responded to this by serving institutional buyers — hospitals, rehabilitation centres, government programmes — rather than individuals directly. The result is devices that are clinically functional, visually institutional, and priced for procurement budgets rather than personal ones.
Tactiq is built on a different premise. Smart ring hardware is a mature and increasingly affordable technology. The sensors required for knuckle tap detection exist in consumer wellness devices already on the market. The Bluetooth connectivity, companion app architecture, and gesture recognition software are all established technologies being applied in a new configuration. This means Tactiq can be produced and sold at a consumer electronics price point — comparable to a pair of wireless earbuds — rather than the medical equipment pricing that currently defines the assistive technology space.
Accessibility should not cost more than a laptop. Tactiq is built to prove that it does not have to.

Who it is for
Primary users — blind and visually impaired people
This is the group for whom Tactiq is most transformative. For a totally blind user, Tactiq removes the single greatest daily friction point of smartphone use — the need to navigate a screen they cannot see. Every action they perform repeatedly becomes a gesture they perform without thinking, faster and more reliably than any voice command or screen reader interaction. The setup process is entirely voice-guided, requiring no visual interaction at any point. The interface itself requires no sight — the hands always know where they are.
Secondary users — elderly people struggling with touchscreens
Fine motor difficulties, reduced vision, and unfamiliarity with gesture-based interfaces make smartphones genuinely difficult for many older adults. Tactiq replaces the precision demands of a touchscreen with the gross motor familiarity of tapping your own fingers — something that requires no training to understand and minimal practice to use comfortably.
Tertiary users — everyone else
Commuters who need phone control while their eyes are on the road or the platform. Students who want to control audio and take shortcuts without breaking focus. Workers who want faster access to repeated actions. Athletes and gym users who cannot easily interact with a phone mid-activity. Anyone who has ever wanted their phone to respond to them without demanding their full attention.
Tactiq does not ask any of these users to identify themselves as disabled, struggling, or in need of accommodation. It is a performance and control device that happens to be life-changing for the people who need it most.

The design principle
Every decision in Tactiq's design answers one question: can a blind user do this entirely on their own?
Setup — voice-guided from first pairing to full customisation. Navigation — the body is the map, proprioception replaces sight. Customisation — profiles named, saved, and swapped by voice. Emergency access — fixed position, fixed gesture, always reachable. Daily use — no screen, no wake word, no failure points.
The rings themselves are designed as consumer objects, not medical devices. Slim, matte, minimal — the kind of thing that sits beside a laptop on a desk and looks like it belongs there. No clinical aesthetic, no accessibility iconography, no signal to the world about why you are wearing them.

The one-line version
Tactiq turns the human hand into a phone controller — wearable, invisible, fully personal, and accessible to anyone who has ever found a touchscreen too demanding and a voice assistant too unreliable.
